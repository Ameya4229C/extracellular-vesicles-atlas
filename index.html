The status of extracellular vesicles as drug carriers and therapeutics

Ameya P. Chaudhari, Omar M. Budayr, Emily E. Bonacquisti, Caden C. Kussatz, Mark S. Bannon, Karissa J. Law, Yusha Liu, Matthew L. Bolton, Patrick M. Glassman, Leaf Huang, Juliane Nguyen

How to interact with these figures:

The figures below are interactive replots of Figures 1–5 from the review. Hover any point for details; on figures with per-study data (clinical trials, loading-capacity studies, siRNA dose-response, mouse biodistribution), click a point to open the source paper or trial registration. Aggregate bars (article counts) are hover-only. To get a closer look, click and hold to draw a box around any region of interest to zoom in — double-click anywhere on the plot to reset the view.

Figure 1 · Preclinical research on extracellular vesicles

a · Research articles 2012–2024 by category

Articles in PubMed mentioning exosome* or extracellular vesicle*, classified into four key categories: diagnostics, therapeutics (and drug carriers), mechanistic roles, and method or protocol development.

b · Articles by donor cell type

Original research articles categorised by EV donor cell type, split into total papers and the subset focused on therapeutic applications. CNS, central nervous system; HEK, human embryonic kidney.

Figure 2 · Clinical research on therapeutic EVs

a · Overview of 338 clinical studies by study aim

338 clinical studies categorised by aim: EVs administered as therapeutics (44%), mechanistic studies (36.5%) and diagnostic studies (19.5%).

b · EV doses administered in clinical trials

Highest single-dose level for every trial that reports a numeric dose, grouped by route of administration. Points are split between particle-based and weight-based reporting (separate panels). Click any point to open the ClinicalTrials.gov registration. Per-kg units are scaled to a 70 kg adult; per-volume units (e.g. EVs/mL) are omitted because the total volume is not consistently reported.

Figure 3 · EVs as drug carriers

a · Drug-loading methods used

Number of studies using each drug-loading method (case-folded so e.g. Sonication and sonication are merged).

c · Loading capacity (%LC) by method and molecule class

Reported loading capacity (% w/w, log scale) for every study with a usable value, grouped by loading method and coloured by molecule class. Click any point to open the source paper.

e · siRNA dose-response reporting

Per-paper detail (98 unique studies). Click any row to open the paper.

Of 98 articles that delivered siRNA via EVs, only a small fraction reports a full dose-response curve or an IC50. The donut summarises this; the table below lists every study so each can be opened individually.

Figure 4 · In vivo biodistribution of EVs in murine models

a · Labelling methods used to track EVs in vivo

Distribution of dye/tag types used across all reviewed biodistribution studies in mice.

b · EV doses used in mice by route of administration

Reported dose per study, grouped by route. Only doses expressed as particle number per mouse (left, log axis) or µg of exosomal protein per mouse (right, linear axis) are shown; doses reported only as a volume (µL/mL), a concentration (particles/mL) or other units are omitted because they cannot be placed on a per-mouse scale. Per-kg and per-body-weight values are scaled to a 25 g mouse. Click any point to open the paper.

c · MSC-EV biodistribution (0–6 h post-IV)

For each organ, every point is one study reporting accumulation of MSC EVs after IV administration. Y-axis is the per-organ signal normalised to the study's combined liver + spleen signal — i.e. organ signal ÷ (liver + spleen) × 100. This places studies reported in different absolute units (photons/s, SNR, %ID/g, …) on the same scale. Click any point to open the paper.

d · HEK-EV biodistribution (0–6 h post-IV)

Same construction as Fig 4c, restricted to HEK-derived EVs. Click any point to open the paper.

Figure 5 · In vivo biodistribution by cell source

a · Biodistribution 2–12 h post-IV, by cell source

Each point is one study × organ. Colour encodes the EV cell source. Y-axis is the per-organ signal normalised to the study's combined liver + spleen signal (organ ÷ (liver + spleen) × 100). Click any point to open the paper.

b · Biodistribution at 24 h post-IV, by cell source

Same construction as Fig 5a, restricted to 24 h time points. Click any point to open the paper.

c · Tumour accumulation: cancerous vs non-cancerous EV source

Each point is one study reporting tumour accumulation of unmodified EVs, with the donor cell binned into cancerous and non-cancerous categories. Click any point to open the paper.

d · Engineered vs native EVs across tumour and MPS organs

Tumour accumulation of native EVs and surface-engineered EVs (peptide-, protein-, small-molecule- and aptamer-conjugated), expressed as % signal from tumour relative to mononuclear phagocyte system (MPS) organs. Click any point to open the paper. Native-EV points are wired to their DOI via the curated cancer / non-cancer source. Engineered-EV DOIs are attributed by rank-matching against the source's computed Tumor / MPS values (best-effort).
